Why Cost Clarity Matters for Your Estimate

If you are building an estimate for an amenity center with sport courts, you already know the problem: surfacing numbers are all over the map. One sub bids $5,000 per court, another bids $12,000, and neither one breaks down what is actually included. You are left guessing which number to carry, and guessing on a bid is how you end up eating costs or losing the job.

This guide is built for GC estimators working on multi-family and amenity center projects in Central Texas. We are going to break down real cost ranges by court type, walk through what drives price variance, and flag the hidden costs that blow up budgets after the contract is signed.

The goal is simple: give you numbers you can actually use when you are building your next bid.

Tennis Court Construction Costs

A standard tennis court is 60' x 120' (including overruns). Full construction from bare site to playable surface involves multiple trades and scopes. Here is what each component typically costs in Central Texas as of 2026.

Tennis Court Cost Breakdown (Per Court)
Component Cost Range
Site Prep & Grading $5,000 – $15,000
Concrete Slab (4–6" post-tension or rebar) $25,000 – $45,000
Acrylic Surface System $7,500 – $15,000
Line Striping $800 – $2,000
Net Posts & Hardware $1,500 – $3,500
Fencing (10' chain link, per court) $8,000 – $20,000
Lighting (LED, per court) $15,000 – $35,000
Total (Full Build) $62,800 – $135,500

Surfacing-Only Scope

If your slab is already poured and you just need surfacing, the relevant line item is $7,500 – $15,000 per tennis court for a professional-grade acrylic system like ATS Sports Acrytech. That includes surface prep, multi-coat acrylic application, and game line striping.

The wide range on surfacing comes down to the system you spec. A single-coat bargain acrylic runs cheaper upfront but wears through in 3–5 years under heavy amenity center traffic. A professional multi-coat system like Acrytech holds up 8–10+ years and eliminates the early resurface cycle. We will cover that tradeoff in detail below.

Pickleball Court Construction Costs

A standard pickleball court is 30' x 60' (including overruns) — roughly one-quarter the footprint of a tennis court. Smaller footprint means lower cost per court, which is why developers are adding 4–8 pickleball courts to amenity packages. The demand is real, and your owner is probably asking for them.

Component Cost Range

Acrylic Surface System $3,500 – $7,000
Line Striping $500 – $1,200
Net Posts & Hardware $800 – $1,800
Fencing (per court) $4,000 – $10,000
Lighting (LED, per court) $6,000 – $15,000
Total (Full Build) $25,300 – $60,000

Multi-Court Pickleball Builds

Most amenity center projects spec 4–8 pickleball courts on a shared slab. When you are pouring one large pad and surfacing multiple courts, the per-court cost drops significantly. For surfacing only on a multi-court slab, budget $3,500 – $7,000 per pickleball court for a professional-grade system.

The cost to install a pickleball court is roughly 40–50% less than tennis on a per-court basis. But watch the total scope — a project with 6 pickleball courts can easily match the total surfacing cost of 2 tennis courts. Make sure your estimate accounts for the full court count, not just the per-court rate.

Multi-Sport Court Costs

Multi-sport courts — typically a tennis court with pickleball lines overlaid, or a dedicated pad with dual-line striping — are increasingly common on amenity center projects. Owners want flexibility without doubling the footprint.

Dual-Line Courts

The most cost-effective approach: a full-size tennis court with pickleball lines added. This gives residents both sports on one slab. The added cost is minimal — striping dual lines adds $500 – $1,500 per court over single-sport striping. The surface system and slab are identical.

Conversion Projects

Converting an existing tennis court to add pickleball lines (or vice versa) is a common scope in amenity center renovations. If the existing surface is in good condition, you are looking at $1,500 – $4,000 per court for restriping and any needed surface touch-up. If the surface needs a full recoat first, add $5,000 – $10,000 for resurfacing before new lines go down.

Restripe existing court for dual sport $1,500 – $4,000
Full resurface + dual-line restripe $6,500 – $14,000

What Drives Cost Variance

You have seen the ranges. Here is what pushes a project toward the high end or the low end of those numbers.

Site Conditions

Rocky or expansive clay soils (common in Central Texas) increase grading and prep costs. Sites with significant slope require more cut-and-fill work. If your geotech report shows problem soils, build in a contingency on the site prep line item.

Concrete Spec

Post-tension slabs run more than standard rebar but reduce cracking risk — a big deal on court surfaces where a hairline crack telegraphs through the acrylic. For amenity centers with high traffic expectations, most specs call for 5" or 6" post-tension. Thinner slabs save money upfront but invite cracking and callbacks.

Surface System Quality

This is where the biggest long-term cost difference lives. A cheap single-coat acrylic might save $2,000–$4,000 per court on day one. But cheap systems break down under Central Texas UV and heat, requiring resurfacing in 3–5 years at $5,000+ per court. A professional multi-coat system like ATS Sports Acrytech costs more upfront but lasts 8–10+ years — no early resurface, no callbacks, no carrying costs from fixing someone else's work.

Number of Courts

Per-court costs decrease on multi-court builds. Mobilization, equipment, and crew costs get amortized across more courts. A 6-court pickleball build will have a lower per-court surfacing cost than a single-court project.

Access and Staging

Tight site access, limited staging areas, or coordination constraints with other active trades can add cost. If your surfacing sub has to work around an active pool construction or limited gate access, that adds time and overhead. Call it out in your bid package so subs can price it accurately.

The Hidden Costs GCs Miss

The bid number on the page is not the real cost. Here are the line items that never make it into the original estimate but show up during construction or within the first few years.

Avg. $5,000 / Court

Callbacks from Cheap Subs

Cheap surface systems peel, blister, and crack within 1–2 years. The callback rate on bargain installs runs 35–40%. That is a $5,000-per-court rework that was never in your original number.

$5,000+ / Court

Early Resurfacing (3–5 Years)

Low-quality acrylic breaks down fast under Central Texas UV. When the property manager calls about peeling courts in year 3, someone is paying for a full resurface. If you carried the cheap sub, that call is coming to you.

~$2,000 / Court

Change Orders

Vague bids lead to vague scopes. When a cheap sub's bid does not itemize surface prep, crack repair, or primer coats, those "extras" show up as change orders mid-project. Average CO on court surfacing: $2,000 per court.

$1,500 / Day

Project Delays

Unreliable subs cause schedule delays that cascade across your other trades. At $1,500/day in GC carrying costs, a 3-day delay from a no-show surfacing crew costs more than the bid savings you got from hiring them.

The cheapest bid is almost never the cheapest project. The real cost is what happens in the 2–5 years after the sub leaves the site.
